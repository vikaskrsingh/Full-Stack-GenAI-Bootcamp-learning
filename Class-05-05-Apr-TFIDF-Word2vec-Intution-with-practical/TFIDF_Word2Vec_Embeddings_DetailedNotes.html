📌 Common Thread Across ALL Methods

Every single technique — OHE, BoW, TF-IDF, Word2Vec — starts with the same first step: build a vocabulary (a set of all unique words from your data). The vocabulary decides the vector dimension. This is universal.

💡 Why Do We Need a Vocabulary?

Text data is unstructured — different sentences have different lengths. For example: "I love India" (3 words) vs "I love generative AI with DL" (6 words). If we create a vector per sentence, they'd have different sizes — models can't handle that.

The solution: extract all unique words across all data → that's the vocabulary → use vocabulary size as the fixed dimension for every vector. Now every sentence maps to the same-size vector. This is why vocabulary matters in every technique.

TF-IDF — Term Frequency × Inverse Document Frequency
The smartest classical encoding — invented by Google, used in their search engine
📖 Definition

TF-IDF is a technique to convert text data into numerical vectors by measuring both how often a word appears in a document (Term Frequency) AND how important/rare that word is across all documents (Inverse Document Frequency).

Final score = TF × IDF. This score becomes the value in the vector for that word position.

This TF-IDF technique was invented by Google. Before the neural network era, TF-IDF was ruling. It is a very famous interview question — I have given Gen AI interviews recently and they asked about TF-IDF. So don't underestimate this.

— Sunny Savita

The Core Intuition

Bag of Words already captured word frequency — but it had one big flaw: it treated common meaningless words ("the", "is", "and") with the same respect as rare, meaningful words ("machine", "transformer"). TF-IDF fixes this by penalizing common words and rewarding rare, informative words.

💡 The Core Idea — Why TF-IDF Is Smarter

Consider these sentences appearing in documents about AI:

"the model is good"   /   "the AI model is fast"   /   "the transformer model learns"

The word "the" appears everywhere → BoW gives it a high count → treated as important. But "the" carries no meaning!

The word "transformer" appears in only one document → TF-IDF gives it a HIGH weight for that document → correctly identified as a meaningful, important word.

TF-IDF = smarter counting that understands informativeness.

The Formulas — Explained from First Principles
Both formulas, why they work, and what every term means

Part 1 — Term Frequency (TF)

📐 Term Frequency Formula
TF(word, sentence) = occurrences of word in sentence ÷ total words in sentence
Measures: "How dominant is this word in THIS specific document?"
✏️ TF Worked Example — Sentence: "My name is Sunny and Sunny teaches AI"

Total words in sentence: 7 (my, name, is, Sunny, and, Sunny, teaches, AI) → wait, let's count: my(1) name(2) is(3) Sunny(4) and(5) Sunny(6) teaches(7) AI(8) = actually depends on cleanup

Simplified by instructor: Total words = 7

How many times does "Sunny" appear? → 2 times

TF("Sunny") = 2 ÷ 7 = 0.286

This number tells us: "Sunny" makes up 28.6% of this sentence — it's dominating, so it's likely important.

✏️ Another TF Example — "AI is Important, AI is Future, AI is Dangerous"

Total words: 9

How many times does "AI" appear? → 3 times

TF("AI") = 3 ÷ 9 = 0.333

Which word is dominating the sentence? AI. TF mathematically proves this — AI has the highest TF score.

Note: "is" also appears 3 times → TF("is") = 3/9 = 0.333 too. But IDF will fix this — "is" appears in many documents so its IDF will be LOW, dragging the final score down.

Part 2 — Inverse Document Frequency (IDF)

📐 Inverse Document Frequency Formula
IDF(word) = log( Total number of documents (D) ÷ number of documents containing the word )
Measures: "How rare / unique is this word across ALL documents?"
🔑 Two Purposes of IDF

Purpose 1: Reduce the weight of common, unimportant words. If "the" appears in every single document → D / D = 1 → log(1) = 0 → IDF score ≈ 0 → TF×IDF ≈ 0. "The" gets almost zero weight.

Purpose 2: Boost the weight of rare but informative words. A word appearing in only 1 out of 1000 documents → IDF = log(1000/1) = 6.9 → very high → that word gets high importance for that specific document.

✏️ IDF Worked Example

Data: 3 sentences total

D1: I love ML
D2: I love DL
D3: I like GenAI

Question: What is IDF("love")?

Total documents (D) = 3

In how many documents does "love" appear? → D1 and D2 → 2 documents

IDF("love") = log(3 ÷ 2) = log(1.5) = ≈ 0.405

Relatively low IDF — "love" appears in most documents so it's not very rare or distinguishing.

Final TF-IDF Score

📐 Final Formula
TF-IDF(word, document) = TF × IDF
TF checks word importance in the current document. IDF checks word importance across all documents. Together they give a meaningful weight.
✓ Summary — TF vs IDF vs TF-IDF

TF alone → can be fooled by common words ("the", "is" appear everywhere)

IDF alone → doesn't consider how frequent the word is in the current document

TF × IDF → best of both: high only when the word is both frequent in THIS document AND rare across ALL documents

Why Is There a Logarithm in IDF?
The most common question from the session — answered completely

Without log, the value could be extreme. Log is keeping everything in a range — a small, manageable range. That is the reason we use log here.

— Sunny Savita

The Problem Without Log

Imagine you have 1,000 documents. Let's calculate IDF for words with different rarity:

Rare word (appears in 1 doc):   IDF = 1000 ÷ 1 = 1000 ← extreme!
Medium word (appears in 10 docs):   IDF = 1000 ÷ 10 = 100 ← still large
Common word (appears in 500 docs):   IDF = 1000 ÷ 500 = 2 ← small

Range WITHOUT log: 2 to 1000 ← massive, unmanageable spread

This massive range (2 to 1000) causes problems in ML calculations. The rare word would dominate the model just because of scale, not because of meaning.

The Solution: Apply Log

log(1000) with base E: 6.91
log(100) with base E: 4.61
log(2) with base E: 0.69

Range WITH log: ~0.69 to 6.91 ← compressed, manageable
📌 Three Types of Logarithm in TF-IDF

log base 2 → value of log₂(10,000) ≈ 13.19

log base 10 → value of log₁₀(10,000) = 4

log base e (natural log, written as ln) → value of ln(10,000) ≈ 9.21

Different implementations use different bases. The key point is that all versions compress the range of the raw ratio. scikit-learn uses log base e by default.

💡 One-Line Answer for Interviews

We use logarithm in IDF to normalize/compress the range of values, preventing extreme IDF values (from very rare words) from dominating the vector calculation.

Complete Worked Example — Building TF-IDF Vectors
Step by step, exactly as taught in class with the same dataset

The Dataset

📄 Four Documents (same data used in class)
D1: people watch cricket
D2: cricket watch cricket (people give command)
D3: people give comment cricket
D4: cricket give command

Step 1 — Vocabulary: Extract all unique words (alphabetically):

[comment, cricket, give, people, watch]

Vocabulary size = 5 → Every document vector will have 5 positions.

Step 2 — Calculate TF for Each Word in D1

D1 = "people watch cricket" → 3 total words

WordIn D1?Count in D1TF = count ÷ 3
commentNo00 ÷ 3 = 0.000
cricketYes11 ÷ 3 = 0.333
giveNo00 ÷ 3 = 0.000
peopleYes11 ÷ 3 = 0.333
watchYes11 ÷ 3 = 0.333

Step 3 — Calculate IDF for Each Word (across all 4 documents)

WordAppears in how many docs?IDF = log(4 ÷ docs)Approx Value
comment1 doc (D3)log(4÷1) = log(4)1.386
cricket3 docs (D1,D2,D4)log(4÷3) ≈ log(1.33)0.288
give2 docs (D2,D4 or D3)log(4÷2) = log(2)0.693
people2 docs (D1,D3)log(4÷2) = log(2)0.693
watch2 docs (D1,D2)log(4÷2) = log(2)0.693

Step 4 — Final TF-IDF Vector for D1

Multiply TF × IDF for each word:

comment:   TF(0.000) × IDF(1.386) = 0.000 ← not in D1, so 0
cricket:   TF(0.333) × IDF(0.288) = ≈ 0.096
give:      TF(0.000) × IDF(0.693) = 0.000 ← not in D1
people:   TF(0.333) × IDF(0.693) = ≈ 0.231
watch:    TF(0.333) × IDF(0.693) = ≈ 0.231

D1 TF-IDF Vector = [0.000, 0.096, 0.000, 0.231, 0.231]
🔍 Reading the D1 Vector

"people" and "watch" score 0.231 — they're in D1 and rare enough to be meaningful.

"cricket" scores only 0.096 — it's in D1 but also in 3 other documents, so IDF penalizes it (less unique to D1).

"comment" and "give" score 0.000 — not in D1 at all.

This is TF-IDF working correctly: words frequent in THIS document AND rare across all documents get the highest scores.

💡 Key Clarification from Class

TF is calculated at the sentence/document level — for each specific document separately.

IDF is calculated at the corpus level — looking across ALL documents. It's the same for every document.

Practical Code — TF-IDF in Python
Complete sklearn implementation as taught in class
Python — TF-IDF with sklearn 
# Step 1: Import TfidfVectorizer from sklearn
# Same pattern as CountVectorizer — sklearn makes it consistent
from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer

# Step 2: Define your documents (raw sentences — no manual tokenization needed)
documents = [
    "people watch cricket",
    "cricket watch cricket",
    "people give comment cricket",
    "cricket give command"
]

# Step 3: Create the TF-IDF object
tfidf_obj = TfidfVectorizer()

# Step 4: fit_transform — learns vocabulary AND calculates TF-IDF in one step
# Returns a sparse matrix of TF-IDF scores
tfidf_vector = tfidf_obj.fit_transform(documents)

# Step 5: View the TF-IDF matrix as readable numbers
print("TF-IDF Matrix:\n", tfidf_vector.toarray())

# Step 6: View each document's vector separately
for vector in tfidf_vector:
    print(vector)

# Step 7: Check the vocabulary (always alphabetically sorted)
print("\nVocabulary:", tfidf_obj.get_feature_names_out())

Code Breakdown — What Each Line Does

LineWhat It Does
TfidfVectorizer()Creates the TF-IDF object — the class that holds all logic
fit_transform(docs)Scans all documents (fit) → builds vocabulary → computes TF, IDF, TF×IDF (transform) → returns matrix
.toarray()Converts sparse matrix to readable NumPy array of decimal numbers
get_feature_names_out()Shows the vocabulary — the column headers for the matrix
🛠️ The sklearn Pattern — Always the Same

All three encoding classes follow the same API: fit() → transform() → or fit_transform()

OHE: OneHotEncoder() → fit_transform(words_2d)

BoW: CountVectorizer() → fit_transform(sentences)

TF-IDF: TfidfVectorizer() → fit_transform(sentences)

TF-IDF — Pros & Cons
Essential for interviews — know both sides thoroughly
✅ Advantages
  • Captures importance, not just count — rare words get higher weight
  • Reduces impact of common words — "the", "is", "and" get near-zero weights
  • Better than Bag of Words for search and retrieval
  • Used historically in Google's search engine for page ranking
  • Works for information retrieval and document ranking
  • No model training required — pure calculation
  • Simple and mathematically interpretable
❌ Disadvantages
  • Ignores word order — "dog bites man" = "man bites dog"
  • No semantic understanding — "love" and "like" look completely different
  • Sparse, high-dimensional vectors — same problem as OHE and BoW
  • Cannot handle context — "bank" (river) vs "bank" (money) treated same
  • Out-of-Vocabulary problem — new words at inference get score of 0
  • Outdated — replaced by modern embedding models in production
📌 TF-IDF's Legacy — Where It Still Matters

BM25 (Best Match 25) is an advanced version of TF-IDF, and it is still actively used today inside RAG systems as the "sparse retrieval" component. You will encounter BM25 in the RAG chapter.

Google's original search engine used TF-IDF for indexing. It would convert queries and web pages to TF-IDF vectors, then perform similarity search to rank results.

Critical Distinction — Encoding vs Embedding
One of the most important conceptual clarifications of the entire module

TF-IDF is an encoding technique, NOT an embedding technique. If any neural network is required, if any transformer network is required — the generated vectors from that are called embeddings. And why do we use the word "embedding"? Because those vectors have semantic meaning.

— Sunny Savita
PropertyEncoding (OHE, BoW, TF-IDF, BM25)Embedding (Word2Vec, BERT, etc.)
Training Required❌ No — pure calculation✅ Yes — neural network training
Semantic Meaning❌ None — "love" ≠ "like"✅ Yes — "love" ≈ "like"
Context Awareness❌ "bank" always same✅ "bank" changes by context
Vector TypeSparse (mostly 0s)Dense (all meaningful values)
EraPre-2013 (classical NLP)2013–present
💡 Simple Rule to Remember

No training → Encoding (OHE, BoW, TF-IDF, BM25)

Neural network training → Embedding (Word2Vec, BERT, GPT embeddings, OpenAI embeddings)

The word "embedding" specifically implies the vector carries semantic meaning — something you can only get from a trained model.

Why Embeddings Are Needed — The Key Limitation Example

🔴 The Problem That Encoding Cannot Solve

Consider these two sentences:

Sentence 1: "I like this movie"
Sentence 2: "I love this film"

Any human would say these mean essentially the same thing. But with OHE / BoW / TF-IDF:

→ "like" ≠ "love" → treated as completely different words
→ "movie" ≠ "film" → treated as completely different words

The two sentences would have zero overlap in their vectors despite being semantically identical.

Embeddings solve this: they learn that "like" ≈ "love" and "movie" ≈ "film" from training data, placing similar words close in vector space.

Word2Vec — Classical Neural Embedding
Google's 2013 research paper that revolutionized NLP
📖 Definition

Word2Vec is a neural network-based method (invented by Google, 2013) to represent words as dense vectors where semantically similar words have similar vectors.

Research paper: "Efficient Estimation of Word Representations in Vector Space" (2013). This paper is in the GitHub repo for this session.

Two Ways to Use Word2Vec

Option 1 — Pre-Trained Model
  • Google already trained it on billions of words
  • You download and use it directly — no training needed
  • Produces 300-dimensional vectors per word
  • Available via Gensim library
  • ✓ This is what the class focuses on
Option 2 — Custom Model
  • You train Word2Vec on YOUR own dataset
  • Useful for domain-specific language (medical, legal)
  • Requires training time and compute
  • Notebook available on GitHub to practice
  • Not covered in depth — this is classical NLP scope

Core Idea: Representing Words as Features

Word2Vec represents a word in terms of features — and those features are being learned from the data itself. The model is trying to find the best possible features to represent the word. That is the fundamental idea.

— Sunny Savita

Instead of 0s and 1s or simple counts, Word2Vec gives each word a vector of learned decimal values — each value representing a latent feature of that word.

🎯 Instructor's Word Feature Analogy — Sunny vs Tiger

Let's say we define 5 features for words: King, Speaks, Runs, Rich, Teacher. We rate each word 0 to 1 for each feature:

FeatureSunny (value)ReasoningTiger (value)Reasoning
King0.0Sunny is not a king0.0Tiger is not a king

📐 Resulting Word Vectors
Vector("Sunny") = [0.0, 1.0, 0.6, 0.3, 1.0]
Vector("Tiger") = [0.0, 0.0, 0.9, 0.0, 0.0]

These two vectors are VERY different → mathematically we can say "Sunny" and "Tiger" are not similar. Correct!

Now imagine doing this for "King" and "Queen" — their vectors would be close in most features → the model would recognize them as similar. That's the semantic power of Word2Vec.

⭐ The Famous Word2Vec Demonstration

Google's trained model can do this:

vector("King") - vector("Man") + vector("Woman") ≈ vector("Queen")

The model learned that "King" and "Queen" have the same relationship as "Man" and "Woman". This is arithmetic on meaning — something no classical encoding can do.

Google's Pre-Trained Model: 300-Dimensional Vectors

📐 Dimensions in Word2Vec

Google trained Word2Vec on Google News data (billions of words). Each word is represented as a 300-dimensional vector. That means each word has 300 learned features.

Unlike OHE where dimension = vocabulary size (could be 50,000+), Word2Vec always uses a fixed, much smaller dimension (100, 200, or 300).

The vectors are dense — all 300 values are meaningful floats, no zeros.

Two Architectures in Word2Vec

📌 CBOW and Skip-Gram — Coming in Next Class

The instructor mentioned two techniques inside Word2Vec: CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words) and Skip-Gram. These will be taught in the next session in detail.

Brief preview: CBOW predicts a target word from surrounding context words. Skip-Gram predicts surrounding context words given a target word.

Why We Learn Word2Vec Even Though It's "Old"

🎯 Three Reasons to Learn Word2Vec

1. Fundamentals: Understanding how neural embeddings work is the foundation for understanding Transformers and LLMs.

2. Interviews: Word2Vec is still asked about in GenAI interviews — shows depth of NLP knowledge.

3. Transformer connection: The initial embedding layer inside Transformer models uses Word2Vec-like concepts. When tokens are converted to embeddings before entering the Transformer, that's Word2Vec concepts in action.

How Word2Vec Uses This

Word2Vec trains a small neural network on a specific task (e.g., "predict the context word given a target word"). After training, we don't use the network for predictions — we extract the learned weight matrix. That weight matrix IS the word embeddings. Each row in the matrix = the embedding vector for one word.

Interview Preparation
From real GenAI interview questions the instructor has personally faced

🎯 Must-Know Interview Questions — TF-IDF & Word2Vec

Q1: What is TF-IDF and why is it better than Bag of Words?
TF-IDF (Term Frequency × Inverse Document Frequency) improves upon BoW by measuring not just how frequently a word appears, but also how important/rare it is across all documents. BoW gives equal credit to common words like "the" and meaningful words like "transformer." TF-IDF penalizes common words (low IDF) and rewards rare, informative words (high IDF). The result is a smarter weighting that better represents what each document is actually about.
Q2: Why is there a logarithm in the IDF formula?
Without a log, IDF values can be extremely large (e.g., 1000/1 = 1000 for a very rare word). This creates an extreme range of values that makes ML calculations unstable. Applying log compresses this range — log(1000) ≈ 6.9 — keeping all IDF values manageable. In ML, we prefer data in small, normalized ranges for stable gradient computation. Log can be base 2, base 10, or natural log (e), all achieving the same normalization effect.
Q3: What is the difference between encoding and embedding?
Encoding (OHE, BoW, TF-IDF, BM25) converts text to numbers using pure calculations — no model training involved. The resulting vectors have no semantic meaning. Embedding uses a trained neural network to create vectors — the model learns from data, so semantically similar words end up with similar vectors. Embeddings are dense, compact, and semantically meaningful. The rule: if training was involved → embedding. If it's just math → encoding.
Q4: What is Word2Vec and how does it work?
Word2Vec is a neural network-based model (Google, 2013) that represents words as dense floating-point vectors. It trains a neural network on a text corpus using either CBOW (predict target word from context) or Skip-Gram (predict context words from target). After training, the learned weight matrix serves as the embedding — each row is the vector for one word. Similar words end up with similar vectors because they appear in similar contexts during training. The Google pre-trained model produces 300-dimensional vectors.
Q5: What are the limitations of TF-IDF?
Five main limitations: (1) Ignores word order — "dog bites man" and "man bites dog" produce same vectors. (2) No semantic understanding — "love" and "like" appear completely unrelated. (3) High-dimensional sparse vectors — dimension equals vocabulary size, mostly zeros. (4) Cannot handle context — "bank" (river) and "bank" (finance) treated identically. (5) Out-of-Vocabulary problem — new unseen words score zero.
Q6: Where is TF-IDF still used today?
TF-IDF's advanced version, BM25, is still actively used in RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation) systems as the sparse retrieval component. BM25 ranks documents by relevance to a query and is the backbone of many search engines. Hybrid RAG systems combine BM25 (sparse) with transformer embeddings (dense) for the best retrieval results. Google used TF-IDF in their early search engine for page indexing and ranking.
Q7: What is an epoch in neural network training?
One epoch = one complete forward propagation (FP) through the entire training dataset + one complete backward propagation (BP). During FP, we calculate the prediction and loss. During BP (using gradient descent), we adjust the weights to reduce the loss. Multiple epochs are run until the model converges to the best possible weights.
